Against the Infinite Scroll

What happens to a magazine when it remembers that it is allowed to have an ending?

Iris LindqvistJune 16, 2026 · 9 min read

Somewhere in the last fifteen years, the page quietly stopped ending. The feed replaced the issue; the scroll replaced the spread. We told ourselves this was generosity — there is always more — but a thing that never ends is also a thing you can never finish. And a reader who can never finish never gets to feel the particular satisfaction of having read.

This magazine is a small argument with that idea. Every issue of RECTO has a back cover. You can hold the whole of it. When you reach the end, the end is there — not as a failure of supply, but as a designed event.

The tyranny of the bottomless page

Infinite scroll was invented to solve a measurable problem: pagination cost clicks, and clicks cost readers. Remove the seam between page one and page two and engagement goes up. The metric was honest. What it could not measure was the feeling on the other side of the glass — the low, ambient sense that the work is never done.

An ending is not a limitation of the medium. It is the most generous thing a page can offer: the permission to stop.

Designers know this instinct from other corners of the craft. A good form tells you when you are finished. A good checkout has a last step. Only reading, of all things, has been redesigned to deny you the curb.

Designing for the last page

When you accept that a thing will end, you start designing the ending on purpose. You pace the middle. You earn the close. The final paragraph stops being where the content happens to run out and becomes a place you were always walking toward.

That is the whole wager of this magazine, really: that a reader given a horizon will walk further than a reader given a treadmill. So far, four issues in, the wager is holding.
